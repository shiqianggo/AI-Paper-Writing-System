### Final Quality Score

### Consensus Analysis

#### Points of Agreement

- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The manuscript addresses an interesting and potentially publishable question at the intersection of invasion ecology, eco-evolutionary differentiation, and multi-omics integration.
- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The central claims are currently stronger than the evidence base supports, especially around **“rapid evolution,” “decoupling,”** and **“phenotypic buffering.”**
- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The integrative Bayesian model is ambitious and valuable in concept, but the inferential strength is constrained by the **final matched sample size of 20** and its imbalance across patches.
- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The reliance on **PC1-only summaries** for spatial, SNP, transcriptomic, and metabolomic layers is a major interpretive limitation, especially given the low variance explained by PC1 in several layers.
- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The metabolomics batch issue is serious and must be handled with fuller transparency and sensitivity discussion.
- **[CONSENSUS-4]** The language of **“whole-plant” or “macroscopic phenotype”** is too broad relative to the actual measured phenotypes, which are mainly aboveground and limited in scope.
- **[CONSENSUS-3]** The manuscript’s strongest defensible contribution is likely not proof of rapid evolution, but rather evidence of **microgeographic differentiation with coordinated leaf-level molecular shifts and weak direct association with measured aboveground phenotypes**.
- **[CONSENSUS-3]** The paper is potentially suitable for a solid ecology/evolution or invasion journal if the claims are tightened and the methods made more transparent.

#### Points of Disagreement

- **[Issue] Whether “rapid evolution” can remain a central framing term**
    
    - **Reviewer positions**:
        - EIC: may be too strong; prefers “rapid microgeographic differentiation” or similar.
        - R1: statistical evidence cannot sustain that claim.
        - R2: domain evidence supports differentiation/sorting, not demonstrated rapid evolution.
        - R3: less central focus, but supports caution.
    - **Editorial resolution**: **Do not retain “rapid evolution” as a headline claim unless substantially softened and explicitly qualified.**
    - **Rationale**: This is an **existence/severity disagreement** about what the data prove. Across both method and domain review, the evidence supports differentiation and layered response, but not a clean demonstration of rapid evolution in the stronger evolutionary sense.
- **[Issue] Whether “decoupling” may remain in the title**
    
    - **Reviewer positions**:
        - EIC: possibly retain only if weakened and narrowly defined.
        - R1: current model does not justify strong decoupling language.
        - R2: weak correspondence does not automatically equal decoupling.
        - R3: acceptable only if reframed as a limited, measured, leaf-omics-to-selected-aboveground-traits mismatch.
    - **Editorial resolution**: **“Decoupling” should not remain as an unqualified title-level conclusion in the current form.**
    - **Rationale**: This is mainly a **severity and definition disagreement**. The strongest supported reading is “weak direct association” or “limited translation to measured aboveground phenotypes,” not definitive system-level decoupling.
- **[Issue] Best journal positioning**
    
    - **Reviewer positions**:
        - EIC: stronger fit if reframed away from hard mechanistic/evolutionary proof claims.
        - R2/R3: more comfortable if pitched toward invasion ecology / eco-evolutionary differentiation rather than a high-bar molecular mechanism narrative.
        - R1: method constraints may limit competitiveness for more demanding molecular ecology outlets.
    - **Editorial resolution**: **The manuscript appears more competitive if framed as an invasion ecology / microgeographic differentiation paper with multi-omics insight, rather than as a definitive molecular ecology mechanism paper.**
    - **Rationale**: This is a **journal-fit disagreement** resolved by evidence and risk. The current study has novelty, but not yet the inferential closure expected for the strongest mechanism-driven venues.

#### DA-Critical Issues

- **[Issue] Small matched sample size is too weak for strong path-style mechanistic claims**
    
    - **DA argument**: n=20, with patch imbalance, is insufficient for the strength of the hierarchical inference being claimed.
    - **Corroboration from other reviewers**: Strongly corroborated by R1; implicitly supported by EIC, R2, and R3.
    - **Editorial assessment**: **Validated DA-CRITICAL issue.**
    - **Required author response**: Recast the model as exploratory/integrative unless stronger validation or sensitivity evidence is added; reduce causal or mechanism-like phrasing throughout.
- **[Issue] PC1-only compression may manufacture or obscure the main conclusion**
    
    - **DA argument**: low-explained-variance PC1 summaries may create the appearance of decoupling.
    - **Corroboration from other reviewers**: Strongly corroborated by R1; partially supported by EIC and R3.
    - **Editorial assessment**: **Validated DA-CRITICAL issue.**
    - **Required author response**: Justify PC1 choice rigorously and add sensitivity analyses using alternative dimensional summaries if possible.
- **[Issue] Rapid evolution is not demonstrated**
    
    - **DA argument**: observed patch differences may reflect founder effects, spatial sorting, clonal structure, or unmeasured environment rather than demonstrated rapid evolution.
    - **Corroboration from other reviewers**: Strong corroboration from EIC and R2; consistent with R3.
    - **Editorial assessment**: **Validated DA-CRITICAL issue.**
    - **Required author response**: Downgrade claim language and clearly separate differentiation, sorting, plasticity, acclimation, and adaptation.
- **[Issue] Chronosequence is over-causalized**
    
    - **DA argument**: space-for-time substitution is being over-interpreted as a time-resolved mechanism.
    - **Corroboration from other reviewers**: Supported by EIC, R1, and R2.
    - **Editorial assessment**: **Validated DA-CRITICAL issue.**
    - **Required author response**: Explicitly state inferential limits of the chronosequence design in Introduction, Results framing, and Discussion.

### Decision Rationale

This manuscript has clear strengths: it asks a timely question, uses a biologically important invasive system, and attempts an ambitious integration of field phenotypes, genomics, transcriptomics, metabolomics, and hierarchical modeling. All core reviewers agree that the study contains a real signal and a publishable nucleus.

However, the review package also shows unusually strong convergence on a different point: **the manuscript’s evidentiary core is currently narrower than its headline claims**. The strongest supported contribution is not a demonstrated mechanism of rapid evolution and phenotypic buffering, but rather a more cautious finding of **microgeographic differentiation accompanied by coordinated leaf-level molecular shifts, with weak direct linkage to the measured aboveground phenotypes included in the model**.

The methodology reviewer’s concerns carry substantial weight here because the manuscript’s central narrative depends heavily on the integrated Bayesian model. The final matched dataset is small and imbalanced; PC1-only compression is severe; metabolomics suffered a major batch-related exclusion; and MCMC/reporting transparency is not yet sufficient for the strength of interpretation currently presented. These concerns do not necessarily invalidate the study, but they do constrain what can be claimed.

Accordingly, the manuscript is **not rejected**, because the editorial board sees genuine novelty and a plausible revision pathway. But it also cannot be advanced as minor revision because the present version requires substantial claim recalibration, methodological clarification, and a more disciplined articulation of inference boundaries.
