**Title**

**Multi-omics integration reveals a decoupling of molecular reprogramming and macroscopic phenotypes during the microgeographic invasion of _Spartina alterniflora_**

**Abstract**

**Background:** The success of invasive plant species depends on complex biological partitioning—ranging from initial genetic sorting to plastic molecular and phenotypic responses. However, because these layers are typically studied in isolation and at macrogeographic scales, the causal linkages from short-term local establishment to multi-omics dynamics and ultimate whole-plant phenotypes remain poorly understood.  
**Objective:** We aimed to disentangle the directed network of dependencies bridging spatial location, baseline genetic architecture, intermediate omics, and macroscopic phenotypes over a rapid, microgeographic invasion timeline.  
**Methods:** Utilizing a spatial-temporal chronosequence of the invasive grass _Spartina alterniflora_ in the Zhangjiang Estuary, China (spanning four sequential patches established from 2012 to 2015), we integrated field phenotyping, population genomics (SNPs), leaf transcriptomics, and untargeted metabolomics. We engineered a two-stage Bayesian hierarchical model to quantify the pathways connecting these biological layers.  
**Results:** We observed rapid spatial genetic divergence and significant phenotypic variance across the 3-to-4-year chronosequence. The Bayesian framework mapped a highly structured signaling cascade: habitat location strictly drove genetic differentiation (P(E>0)=1_P_(_E_>0)=1) and synchronized transcriptomic behaviors, which in turn tightly regulated downstream metabolomic shifts (P(E>0)=0.997_P_(_E_>0)=0.997). Strikingly, despite this robust molecular reprogramming, the coefficients translating intermediate transcriptomic and metabolomic variances directly into structural biomass or spikelet production were remarkably weak or mixed (e.g., Metabolome →→ Leaf biomass, P(E>0)=0.212_P_(_E_>0)=0.212).  
**Conclusion:** We provide evidence for an "asynchrony" or decoupling between leaf-level omics and cumulative plant phenotypes. This suggests a modular expansion strategy where _S. alterniflora_ leverages sensitive transcriptomic and metabolomic adjustments for immediate micro-environmental stress acclimation without restricting cumulative, polygenic macroscopic investments. This phenotypic buffering mechanism highlights an exceptional evolutionary flexibility facilitating rapid marsh colonization.

**Keywords**

_Spartina alterniflora_; biological invasion; multi-omics integration; Bayesian hierarchical model; phenotypic buffering; microgeographic variation; transcriptomic-metabolomic decoupling
